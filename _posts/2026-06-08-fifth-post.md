## <span style="color:#2AA198;">The Unseen Intermission</span>

---

June 8, 2026


In my last post, we walked through exactly what to expect during your first hearing appointment. We talked about the beeps, the speech tests, 
the diagrams, and the clinical data. I wrapped it up with a gentle reminder to take your time processing everything because there is no rush.

Initially, I planned to use this next post to jump straight into the second appointment—the physical fitting, the programming, 
and the technology of hearing devices.

__But as I sat down to write, I realized something incredibly significant was missing.__

We often talk about the clinical steps as a neat, linear checklist: *Test, Diagnose, Fit, Follow-up*.
But human emotion doesn't live in a straight line.
Between the moment you walk out of that first appointment with an audiogram and the moment you decide to do something about it, 

__there is a heavy, quiet space.__



I  call it the Unseen Intermission.



After your first hearing appointment, you are dropped into this space. 
It is the heavy, quiet environment between receiving a diagnosis and deciding how to live with it.


If you are an adult receiving this news for the first time, you might be asking: _Is this my new normal? How do I mentally prepare for this?_


But for some of us, the diagnosis came when we were too young to remember it. My own hearing loss was discovered when I was just a baby.
I didn’t have a moment where a doctor told me my hearing was permanently gone. Instead, my intermission lasted for years, stretching across my entire childhood.

If you are standing in the quiet aftermath of a fresh diagnosis, or if you have been struggling in silence for years, there is something you need to know: 

__there is no right or wrong way to feel.__



## <span style="color:#2AA198;">The Anger, The Loneliness, and The Drive for Perfection</span>



Growing up, I constantly felt completely out of place. 
I felt like I didn't belong, like there was something fundamentally wrong with me, but I couldn't figure out what it was. 
I was trapped in a deep, isolated loneliness.


I didn't understand why I was always getting so angry—especially with my parents. 
I didn't understand why I couldn't be like my brother, an extrovert who made friends effortlessly. 
Why was I so different from people my age, at school, at home, anywhere?

_Why am I like this?_

_Do I deserve to be here?_

_Do I belong?_

_Am I good enough?_



To cope with those terrifying questions, I tried to be as "perfect" as possible. 
I thought if I could just be flawless, no one would notice the invisible gap between me and the rest of the world.




## <span style="color:#2AA198;">Is This Beneficial or Harmful?</span>


When you are in the thick of this emotional friction, it feels entirely harmful. 
It leads to a spiral of self-doubt, overthinking, and heavy guilt. 
You worry that you are a burden to your family, or you feel deep embarrassment when you miss a word or a punchline.


But confronting these ugly, heavy feelings is actually the most beneficial thing you can do. 
__You cannot rush acceptance.__
Whether you are processing a new audiogram or unearthing years of childhood frustration, 
this emotional friction is a necessary step toward healing.

For me, the turning point didn't happen until I got to college. 
That was the environment where I finally started to learn more, not just of myself but other people. 
I realized that it is okay to have a loss. 


**It is okay to be different.**




## <span style="color:#2AA198;">Shifting from Burden to Awareness</span>

I still struggle today. Hearing loss isn't a problem you solve once and forget about; it is a daily navigation. 
But the difference now is _awareness_.


I am learning how to navigate my world without feeling guilty for who I am. 
I am shedding the old skin of embarrassment and the fear of being a burden. 
I aim to use my personal story to help others with their own hearing loss stories.


If you are preparing for your second appointment or if you are just trying to build up the courage to address your hearing health, 

REMEMBER THIS: **You are not a burden. Your struggle is real, your exhaustion is valid, and you deserve to be an active participant in your own life.**



We don't need to be perfect to belong. We just need to give ourselves permission to take the next step.

Next time, we will finally talk about that second appointment: the fitting. 
But we aren't going to look at it as a clinical task. 
We are going to look at how stepping into that appointment can be a profound act of validation for yourself, and a triumph for the version of you that has struggled in silence for far too long.


Stay tuned, and remember: you belong here. 💜
